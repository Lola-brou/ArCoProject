LLM‑Assisted Ontology Enrichment for Cultural Heritage KGs

A case study using ArCo and the Okame Mask to transform sparse records into semantically rich cultural representations.

Introduction


Cultural heritage knowledge graphs like ArCo (Archival Resource Cultural Ontology) face significant gaps in semantic richness—many artifacts are described with generic metadata that obscures their unique histories. This project demonstrates how LLM-assisted ontology enrichment can bridge these gaps, using the Okame mask (a Japanese folk artifact in ArCo currently labeled only as "MASCHERA"@it) as a case study. By combining SPARQL querying, prompt engineering we transform sparse records into semantically rich cultural representations.

Our investigation began with the broad Cultural Property class in the ArCo knowledge graph to ensure a comprehensive understanding of how artifacts are documented across different categories. This top-level class encompasses all cultural heritage items in the dataset, from musical instruments to ritual objects, allowing us to systematically evaluate metadata quality. By starting with this wide scope, we could identify systemic patterns—such as inconsistent subclass assignments or overly generic labeling—that might be missed with a narrower initial approach.

As we analyzed the data, we noticed that certain masks—often labeled simply as "MASCHERA" (Italian for "mask")—exhibited particularly sparse contextual information. This lack of detail, combined with masks’ rich cultural diversity (spanning Japanese Noh theater, Venetian Carnival, and other traditions), made them an ideal candidate for enrichment. We refined our SPARQL queries to isolate mask-related records, filtering for Italian-language labels to maintain consistency with ArCo’s primary metadata. The results confirmed that most mask entries lacked even basic contextual descriptors (e.g., origin, function, or symbolism), highlighting a critical gap in the knowledge graph.

Choosing masks—and specifically the Okame mask—allowed us to demonstrate how LLM-assisted enrichment could transform minimal records into richly annotated entries. The Okame mask’s clear visual documentation, museum provenance, and layered cultural significance (as a symbol of joy and fertility in Japanese folklore) provided a verifiable "ground truth" for testing our methodology. By starting with the expansive Cultural Property class and then narrowing to masks, we ensured our approach could be adapted to other underserved artifact types, addressing ArCo’s metadata challenges at scale.

Objectives

  1. Semantic Gap Analysis: Identify underspecified properties—specifically the Okame mask—in arco:DemoEthnoAnthropologicalHeritage via systematic SPARQL queries.
  2. LLM‑Guided Enrichment: Generate RDF for missing typology (arco:hasTypology), cultural context (arco:hasCulturalContext), descriptions (dc:description), and multilingual labels (rdfs:label).
  3. Employ LLMs to support and automate RDF generation.
  4. Demonstrate Few‑Shot, Zero‑Shot, and Chain‑of‑Thought prompting for cultural data reasoning.
  5. Robust Validation: Ensure technical correctness (EasyRDF) and cultural accuracy (Museo delle Civiltà).
  6. Evaluate limitations when suggested entities are absent from the KG.
